TRACK 2 — BIG SCOPE ARCHITECTURE: "TRUST LAYER"
================================================================================
A multi-product ecosystem for verifiable product authenticity,
built on top of SICPA's physical security codes.
================================================================================


================================================================================
SECTION 1: CORE PROBLEM & TECHNICAL CONSTRAINT
================================================================================

The Central Tension:
  Good UX requires simplicity. SICPA's technology requires precision.
  These are in direct conflict, and this conflict defines the product.

SICPA Code Technology (confirmed pre-event research):
  - "Money-grade" codes: Copy Detection Patterns (CDP). Confirmed by academic
    literature and SICPA's own publications.
  - NOT standard QR codes. Authenticity is encoded in micro-texture and
    microscopic print variations — a perfect copy is physically impossible.
    The "information loss principle": every print/scan cycle degrades the
    pattern; a counterfeit CDP has measurably less entropy than the original.
  - Verification requires capturing fine detail: sharp focus, good lighting,
    potentially multiple angles (each angle reveals different signal layers).
  - Standard casual phone-snapshots will likely FAIL to verify reliably.
  - CDP can be embedded inside a standard-looking QR code structure to
    also carry traceability data (product ID, batch, etc.).
  - Deep learning attacks on CDP are a known and growing threat — this is
    an active research area, not a solved problem.
  - Open question: does SICPA's SDK handle multi-angle capture internally,
    or does the app need to orchestrate it?
  - Open question: what is the minimum acceptable hardware? Mid-range phone?
    Flagship only? What about older hardware?
  - HACKATHON NOTE: No SICPA API will be available on the day. Participants
    must mock the verification response. This means the UX layer is what
    is actually being evaluated, not the backend integration.

Implication for UX:
  We cannot finalize the user journey until we understand the scan complexity.
  The UX layer must be designed around the SICPA capture requirements,
  not the other way around. This is the #1 technical risk of the product.

Bulk Scanning:
  Almost certainly not feasible at the consumer level with optical SICPA codes.
  CDP requires per-item close-up capture. A store doing inventory at scale
  would need specialized hardware (industrial scanners, camera rigs).
  At consumer phone level: one item at a time is the realistic assumption.
  NFC: SICPA excludes NFC from their own codes (cost + elderly UX concerns).
  However, SICPA explicitly told hackathon participants that NFC-based solutions
  or approaches not using SICPA codes at all are welcome — they are open to
  alternative verification vectors.

SECTION 2: ECOSYSTEM ACTORS & MOTIVES
================================================================================

LAYER 1 — INFRASTRUCTURE

  SICPA
    Role:    Provides physical security codes (CDP inks), verification backend,
             and enterprise track-and-trace platform (SICPATRACE® Evo).
    Wants:   Broad adoption of their codes by manufacturers. More codes in
             circulation = more licensing revenue. Consumer apps are a demand
             driver, not a direct revenue source for them.
    Fears:   Being bypassed by cheaper alternatives (e.g. standard QR +
             blockchain). Complexity of their codes becoming a UX barrier
             that kills consumer adoption before it starts.
    Gives us: Code samples, technical constraints. No live API on hackathon day.

    What SICPA already has (confirmed by research):
      - SICPATRACE® Evo: mature B2G/B2B track-and-trace for tobacco, pharma,
        cannabis, food. Deployed in multiple countries.
      - Regional consumer apps: DGDA Connect (Congo), GRS-SICPA Connect
        (Georgia), CalOrigin (California cannabis), Hakiki (Tanzania).
        All are government-mandated, single-category, country-specific
        white labels of the same backend. None are general-purpose or
        consumer-desirable — people use them because law requires it.
      - Digital Trust Suite: newer layer based on W3C Verifiable Credentials,
        DIDs, OpenID4VC. Targeted at digital identity, not physical scanning.
        Public API at api.dip.sicpa.com / docs.dip.sicpa.com.

    What SICPA does NOT have:
      - A universal, cross-category consumer app anyone would install voluntarily.
      - A network-effect / trust aggregation layer (each scan is discarded after
        verification — zero collective intelligence generated).
      - A guided capture UX designed around CDP's precision requirements.
      - Passive trust for users who cannot or will not scan.
      - A solution for the elderly / low-literacy / low-connectivity segments.

  Regulators / Governments
    Role:    Define compliance requirements (EU Battery Regulation, pharma
             traceability directives, food safety laws, etc.).
    Wants:   Verifiable supply chain data, fraud detection, consumer protection.
    Fears:   Enforcement gaps; systems that look compliant but aren't.
    Relationship: They create the mandate that pushes manufacturers to adopt
             SICPA codes. They are not app users, but they are the reason the
             whole ecosystem exists.

LAYER 2 — SUPPLY CHAIN

  Legitimate Goods Manufacturers
    Who:     Pharma companies, food brands, luxury goods, electronics OEMs,
             aviation parts suppliers.
    Wants:   Proof of authenticity for their products. Brand protection.
             Regulatory compliance. Competitive differentiation ("scan to verify").
    Fears:   Counterfeits damaging their brand reputation and customer health.
    Pays:    SICPA for codes on packaging/products.
    App role: May have a manufacturer dashboard to monitor scan activity,
             geographic distribution of fakes detected, etc.

  Counterfeit Goods Producers
    Who:     Adversaries. Black market manufacturers, organized crime.
    Wants:   Maximum profit with minimum detection.
    Fears:   Consumers scanning and discovering fakes. Stores losing reputation
             for selling fakes. Regulators getting scan data to locate sources.
    Behavior: Will attempt to copy codes (physically impossible with CDP),
             or to flood markets in regions with low scan density.
    Design implication: The "Whistleblower" flow (user finds a fake and reports
             it) is valuable precisely because it maps counterfeit geography.

  Distributors / Wholesalers (Mid-Chain)
    Role:    Move goods between manufacturer and store.
    Wants:   Not to be caught holding counterfeit stock. Chain of custody proof.
    Fears:   Liability if fakes pass through their hands.
    App role: Potentially a B2B scanning workflow — "verify this pallet before
             accepting delivery." Bulk-ish scanning at this layer is more
             feasible (controlled environment, dedicated hardware possible).

LAYER 3 — POINT OF SALE

  Store Owners / Retailers
    Who:     Independent pharmacies, grocery stores, specialty retailers,
             online marketplaces.
    Wants:   Customer trust → repeat business. Protection from liability
             (selling a fake medicine could mean lawsuits). "Certified store"
             badge as marketing asset.
    Fears:   Unknowingly stocking counterfeits. Losing customers to distrust.
    App role: Store-facing dashboard. See their own authenticity rating.
             Optionally: staff scanning workflow for incoming stock.
             The "Store Trust Score" passive feature serves them directly.
    Key insight: Store owners are highly motivated B2B users. They will
             actively encourage scanning in their store if it benefits them.

SECTION 3: END CONSUMER USER GROUPS
================================================================================

The fundamental split: Active Scanners vs. Passive Trust Recipients.
Most people will never scan. They benefit from those who do.

--- GROUP A: YOUNG ADULTS (18–35), TECH-NATIVE ---

  Profile:  Comfortable with QR codes, apps, gamification, social sharing.
  Motivation:
    - Curiosity and fun ("what does this do?")
    - Gamification: scores, streaks, bounties
    - Social: contributing to a visible trust network
    - Health/safety for premium or risky products (supplements, street food)
  Barriers:  Low patience. If scanning takes >5 seconds or fails twice, they stop.
  Scanning frequency: High, but only if the experience is rewarding.
  Design for: Fast capture, instant feedback, satisfying success animation,
             social proof ("you're the 3rd person to scan this today").
  Key role in ecosystem: They are the "1%" who generate the trust data that
             the other 99% passively consume.

--- GROUP B: MIDDLE-AGED ADULTS (35–55), PRAGMATIC ---

  Profile:  Use smartphones fluently but not enthusiastically. Motivated by
             practical safety concerns: medication for children, food safety,
             expensive purchases.
  Motivation:
    - Peace of mind for high-stakes items (medicine, baby food, electronics)
    - Does not need gamification — a clear "AUTHENTIC ✓" result is enough
  Barriers:  Will not tolerate confusing UI or repeated scan failures.
             No interest in leaderboards or social features.
  Scanning frequency: Occasional, high-intent (specific products they care about).
  Design for: Minimal UI, single clear outcome screen, no distractions.
             The UI should almost disappear — just the scan and the result.

--- GROUP C: ELDERLY USERS (65+), PASSIVE ---

  Profile:  Variable smartphone literacy. Most cannot reliably scan even a
             standard QR code without assistance. Scanning a SICPA code
             (requiring precise angle, distance, lighting) is extremely unlikely.
  Motivation:  Health safety (medicines, food), but access to technology is
             the blocker.
  Realistic behavior:
    - Will NOT directly use a scanning app in most cases.
    - WILL benefit from the "Trusted Store" halo passively — children or
      grandchildren find them trusted pharmacies, trusted food stores.
    - Social trust transfer: "My grandson told me this pharmacy is verified,
      I trust it."
  Design for: Do not design a direct scanning flow for elderly. Design the
             Trusted Store discovery feature so that family members can use
             it on behalf of elderly relatives.
  Key insight: The generational trust-relay is how elderly users participate
             in this ecosystem. The product should acknowledge and support
             this relay rather than trying to force direct usage.

--- GROUP D: HIGH-RISK POPULATION IN LOW-INCOME CONTEXTS ---

  Profile:  Most vulnerable to counterfeit medicines. Often in markets where
             1 in 10 medicines is substandard (WHO data). Smartphone penetration
             may be lower; data connectivity may be limited.
  Motivation:  Life-safety. Verification of medicine authenticity.
  Barriers:  Device quality (older phones may not meet camera requirements
             for SICPA CDP scanning). Connectivity. Language/literacy.
  Design for: Offline-capable result caching. Low-bandwidth mode. The store
             reputation layer is especially valuable here — if trusted stores
             are certified, individuals don't need to scan at all.

--- GROUP E: COLLECTORS / ENTHUSIASTS ---

  Profile:  Verify high-value collectibles: trading cards, limited edition
             goods, luxury items, authenticated memorabilia.
  Motivation:  Financial — fakes cost them money. Strong personal motivation.
  Scanning frequency: High, deliberate, patient. Will tolerate complex capture
             flow if it gives reliable results.
  Design for: "Expert mode" — show confidence scores, scan quality metrics,
             detailed provenance data. This group wants transparency.

SECTION 4: PRODUCT COMPONENTS (BIG SCOPE)
================================================================================

PRODUCT 1 — CONSUMER APP ("PROVE")
  The primary consumer-facing scanning experience.
  Sub-flows:
    a) Quick Scan: point, capture, result. Minimal UI.
    b) Guided Scan: haptic + visual guidance for difficult codes (SICPA CDP).
    c) Report a Fake: "Whistleblower" flow — location-tagged counterfeit report.
    d) Store Discovery: map/list of nearby stores with high trust ratings.
    e) Scan History: personal log of verified items.
    f) Trust Credits: gamification layer (optional, non-blocking).

PRODUCT 2 — STORE TRUST DASHBOARD (B2B, RETAILER)
  Web + mobile dashboard for store owners.
  Features:
    a) Real-time trust score (aggregated from consumer scans of their products).
    b) Fake detection alerts: "3 counterfeit reports flagged at your location."
    c) "Verified Store" badge/certificate — physical + digital.
    d) Staff scanning tool: verify incoming stock at delivery.

PRODUCT 3 — MANUFACTURER ANALYTICS PORTAL (B2B, BRAND)
  For legitimate manufacturers using SICPA codes.
  Features:
    a) Geographic heat map of scan activity.
    b) Counterfeit detection geography — where are fakes appearing?
    c) Scan success rate — code quality monitoring.
    d) Regulatory compliance reports.

PRODUCT 4 — SCAN ENGINE / SDK (TECHNICAL CORE)
  The reusable capture + verification layer.
  Handles: video buffer capture, frame quality assessment, SICPA API call,
  result parsing. This is what makes or breaks the entire ecosystem.
  Everything else is UI on top of this.

  Reality check: SICPA's backend is NOT a clean REST API. It is embedded in
  their white-label regional apps. There is no public "send image → get result"
  endpoint. The hackathon requires mocking this response — which means we are
  effectively designing what the API contract should look like, which is itself
  valuable IP for SICPA.

  Key open questions (to resolve at hackathon with SICPA):
    - What angles/lighting does CDP verification require?
    - Is verification online-only or can results be cached offline?
    - What is the expected API response time in production?
    - What confidence thresholds define AUTHENTIC vs. SUSPICIOUS vs. FAKE?
    - Will SICPA provide physical CDP code samples to scan against?


================================================================================
SECTION 5: TRUST AGGREGATION MODEL
================================================================================

Individual scans are raw material. Aggregated, they become infrastructure.

Store Trust Score:
  - Input: anonymized scan results from all users at a given location.
  - Outputs: 0–100 score, displayed passively to users nearby.
  - Decay: recent scans weighted higher; stale data decays.
  - Threshold: minimum N scans required before a score is shown
    (prevents gaming with few fake scans).

Geographic Counterfeit Map:
  - Aggregated "fake detected" reports, anonymized by area (not exact location).
  - Value: regulators, brands, distributors — not directly consumer-facing.

Trust Credit Economy:
  - Scan = earn credits. High-quality scan = bonus. Fake discovered = bounty.
  - Credits could unlock: premium scan history, store badges, partner discounts.
  - Risk: gamification can be gamed. Anti-abuse design required.


================================================================================
SECTION 6: KEY RISKS & OPEN QUESTIONS
================================================================================

1. SICPA SCAN COMPLEXITY (HIGHEST RISK)
   Can a standard smartphone camera reliably capture a CDP code well enough
   for verification? What is the failure rate in real-world conditions?
   If failure rate is >20%, the entire consumer UX collapses.

2. API LATENCY
   If verification takes 3+ seconds, the UX must handle waiting gracefully.
   If it takes 10+ seconds, the product is dead on arrival for casual users.

3. OFFLINE / LOW CONNECTIVITY
   Can any part of verification work offline? Critical for LMIC markets.

4. CODE PLACEMENT ON PRODUCTS
   A code buried under cellophane, on a curved surface, or in a dark corner
   of packaging makes camera capture extremely hard. Who controls this?
   Manufacturers do — and they may not prioritize scanability.

5. FALSE POSITIVE RATE
   An authentic item flagged as fake, or a fake passing as authentic.
   Both destroy trust in the system. What are SICPA's published error rates?

6. ADOPTION CHICKEN-AND-EGG
   Consumers won't scan if few products have codes.
   Manufacturers won't add codes if consumers don't scan.
   SICPA is the entity that breaks this loop — they need enterprise clients first.

7. NO CONSUMER DESIRABILITY IN EXISTING SICPA APPS
   All of SICPA's current consumer apps exist because governments mandate them.
   Nobody installs them by choice. This is the core UX gap and the primary
   opportunity for a hackathon submission to add real value.

8. DEEP LEARNING ATTACKS ON CDP
   Academic literature confirms that neural networks can now generate plausible
   fake CDPs that fool original authentication schemes. This is an arms race.
   Any product built on CDP must assume adversaries will eventually adapt.
   The trust aggregation network (many scans, cross-validation) is a structural
   defense: a single sophisticated fake may fool the algorithm, but anomalous
   scan patterns across a location will surface it.
